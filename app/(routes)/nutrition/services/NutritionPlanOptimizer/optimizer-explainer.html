Nutrition Plan Optimizer · Visual Explainer

Picking how much of each food to eat,
as a math problem

The optimizer answers one question: given a pantry of candidate foods and a set of macro targets, how many grams of each food should the day contain so the totals land as close to the targets as possible? Underneath, that's a constrained discrete optimization problem. This page builds up the math piece by piece, with widgets you can poke at.

1The problem, stated as math

Strip away the food labels and you have a textbook optimization problem with three ingredients:

Decision variables

For each food i, choose a daily quantity qi (in grams / servings). These are the knobs the optimizer turns.

Objective

Minimize a penalty score measuring how far the day's macro totals sit from the targets. Lower = better. Defined in macroScorer.ts.

Constraints

Each qi must come from a discrete set of valid quantities, and at most one food per category may be used. From foodBoundsCalculator.ts.

Every food contributes macros in proportion to how much you eat. If a food's reference serving is amount grams with cal/protein/carbs/fat macros, then q grams contributes (q / amount) of each. The day's totals are just the sum across all foods:

totals.calories = Σi (qi / amounti) · cali // same for protein, carbs, fat

That linear relationship is the entire bridge between "grams of food" and "grams of macros." The hard part isn't computing totals — it's searching the astronomically large space of quantity combinations for the one that scores best. Everything below is about how the score is shaped and how the search is run.

The pipeline at a glance. The optimizer runs in three phases, orchestrated by nutritionPlanOptimizer.ts:
PHASE 1

What's allowed?

Enumerate each food's valid daily quantities given per-meal limits and the number of meals. foodBoundsCalculator

PHASE 2

How much of each?

Search for the quantities whose macro totals score best. dailyQuantityOptimizer

PHASE 3

Which meal?

Spread the daily totals across meal slots (balance calories, carbs pre-workout). mealAllocator

2The score: a weighted, squared penalty

The objective is a weighted sum of squared errors — the same idea as least-squares regression. For each macro, take how far the actual total is from the target (Δ = actual − target), square it (so being off in either direction hurts, and being way off hurts disproportionately), and multiply by a weight. Add a special penalty for dipping below a hard floor. Sum it all up:

score = w.cal · Δcal² + w.prot · Δprot² + w.fat · Δfat² + w.carb · Δcarb² + w.floor · (floorDeficit)²

Two things make this more than plain least-squares, and they're what let one formula express "cutting" vs "bulking" vs "maintenance":

  • The weights are per-macro and asymmetric. Each macro has a separate weight for being over target vs under target. The code picks calDelta < 0 ? caloriesBelow : caloriesAbove for each macro.
  • Squaring means the penalty curve is a parabola: tiny misses barely register, large misses dominate. The optimizer naturally fixes the biggest problem first.

3Asymmetry & floors: how a phase gets its personality

The whole reason for separate above/below weights is that nutrition goals are directional. When cutting, overshooting protein is fine but undershooting is bad; overshooting carbs is bad but hitting the calorie ceiling matters most. The penalty curve becomes a lopsided parabola — gentle on the "good" side, steep on the "bad" side. Here are the actual weights from macroScorer.ts:

macroCutting (below / above)Bulking (below / above)Maintenance (below / above)
calories10 / 1010 / 1010 / 10
protein5 / 215 / 34 / 4
fat15 / 315 / 30 / 0
carbs0 / 00 / 03 / 3
below-floor15 (squared, applied to summed deficit beneath any hard floor)

The penalty curve for one macro

Each side of the dip can have a different steepness. A weight of 0 makes that whole side flat — the optimizer stops caring about that direction entirely (that's why cutting can pour spare calories into carbs for free).

penalty dashed line = target · purple = hard floor
Floors are a separate hinge. A floor only bites when you're below it, so its penalty is max(0, floor − actual)² — flat above the floor, a steep wall below. This lets a macro have a hard minimum without dragging the target around. The floors come from RP rules in nutritionPlanCalculator.ts (e.g. cutting keeps protein ≥ 1.0 g/lb even though the target is 1.2).

4Phase 1: which daily quantities are even allowed?

Before searching, the optimizer figures out the discrete set of valid daily quantities for each food. You can't eat a continuous river of peanut butter — portions come in steps, each meal has a min and max serving, and there are only so many meals. So a daily quantity q is valid only if it can be split into at most numMeals portions, each a whole number of steps within [perMealMin, perMealMax].

q = n · step is valid ⇔ ∃ k ∈ [1, numMeals] such that k·minUnits ≤ n ≤ k·maxUnits // k = how many meals the food appears in; from foodBoundsCalculator.isFeasible

Each ● is a valid daily total. The first valid amount is always reachable in one meal; higher amounts need more meals to hold them.

Phase 1 also bakes in two more rules: required foods drop the 0 option (so they can't be excluded), and a category of mutually-exclusive foods is handled later as a group where at most one member is "on." These valid sets become the only positions the Phase-2 search is ever allowed to stand on.

5Phase 2: the search landscape

Now the real work. Each food sits on its own grid of valid quantities; a "plan" is one choice per food. The score turns that grid of combinations into a landscape — a surface where height is the penalty and we're hunting for the lowest valley. With many foods this surface lives in too many dimensions to picture, so here it is for just two foods (a protein source and a carb source) on top of a fixed backdrop of other foods.

Why is this hard enough to need a clever search? Three reasons, all visible above:

  • It's discrete. You can only stand on grid points. The continuous best might sit between two servings, and snapping to the nearest one can overshoot.
  • It's combinatorial. Two foods make a readable picture; a real plan has a dozen foods, each with many quantities — billions of combinations. Brute force is hopeless.
  • It has plateaus. When a phase zero-weights a macro, huge regions score identically. An exact solver has to rule them out one by one (slow); a naïve climber wanders them aimlessly.

The solution in dailyQuantityOptimizer.ts is a deterministic multi-start local search (hill-climbing). The idea: drop several climbers at carefully chosen starting points, let each walk downhill to the bottom of its valley, and keep the best result. Hill-climbing only finds the lowest point near where it starts, so the variety of starts is what reaches the global best.

The starting plans · startingPlanBuilder.ts

  • all-min — every food at its smallest amount (a calorie-light corner).
  • all-max — every food maxed (a calorie-heavy corner).
  • relaxed guess — a smart seed with the protein/carb balance about right (next section).
  • per-food emphasis — the guess with one food cranked to max, one such start per food, to surface structurally different plans.

Why multi-start never hurts

Refinement can only lower a start's score. So every extra start is a free extra lottery ticket: it either finds a better valley or it doesn't, but it can't make the answer worse. The cost is just more climbs — and each climb is milliseconds. That's the whole bargain that makes this fast and reliable.

6The relaxed guess: cheat the discreteness, then snap back

The all-min and all-max corners are easy to generate but they're terrible starting points for getting macro ratios right. So relaxedGuess.ts builds a smarter seed by solving an easier version of the problem: pretend any fractional quantity is allowed (drop the discrete grid) and ignore the category rules. This "relaxation" removes the discreteness trap entirely.

It solves the relaxed problem with coordinate descent: hold every food fixed but one, find that one food's best amount, move on, and repeat sweeping over all foods until nothing changes. The key fact that makes this clean: with all other foods fixed, the penalty is convex (single-dipped) in one food's amount — so its minimum can be found by ternary search, repeatedly shrinking the bracket toward the dip.

Ternary search on a convex slice

This is the penalty as a function of one food's amount, everything else fixed — a convex bowl. Ternary search probes two interior points, throws away the third that can't contain the minimum, and repeats. Step through it:

After ~40 steps the bracket is microscopic; the midpoint is the food's optimal amount. Then nearestQuantityIndex snaps it back to the closest real serving on the grid.

Why relax at all? At the calorie target, the calorie penalty's slope is zero, so in the continuous world nudging in a little more protein is a pure win. But a whole discrete serving would overshoot calories and get rejected. The relaxed solver finds that sweet protein/carb balance the discrete corners can't reach; rounding it to the grid lands the multi-start search right next to the answer, and the refinement that follows cleans up any rounding or category conflict.

7The two hill-climbing moves

Each climber refines its plan with two kinds of moves, sweeping until a full pass changes nothing (localSearch.ts). Plain ±1-step hill-climbing would stall constantly; these two move types are designed to not stall.

Move #1 — whole-range single move

Re-pick one food across its entire range

For each food (or category group), try every valid amount with the rest of the plan fixed, and jump straight to the best one. Because it considers the food's whole range at once — not just one step up or down — it never stalls a step short of a better amount.

for each group g: freeze everyone else → backdrop pick the amount of g that minimizes score(backdrop + g)

Move #2 — paired two-food move

Adjust two foods together

For every pair of groups, find the best joint setting of both at once. This makes trades a single move can't: when you're short on protein but already at the calorie ceiling, adding whey alone overshoots calories. The fix is "add the protein source and drop a carb filler" in one move — only a two-food move can express it.

for each pair (a, b): freeze everyone else → backdrop pick amounts of a AND b that jointly minimize score

Both moves are made cheap by running-totals bookkeeping (macroMath.ts): instead of re-summing the whole plan, the search subtracts the moving group's contribution to get a fixed "backdrop," then scores each candidate by adding back just four numbers (cal/protein/carbs/fat). Pair moves are capped so a giant two-food product can't blow up the scan — when a pair is too big, the larger food is windowed around its current amount rather than skipped, so every pair still gets a joint move.

8Phase 3: spreading the day across meals

Phase 2 outputs daily quantities. mealAllocator.ts decides which meal each portion lands in. This is a smaller balancing problem, not a re-optimization of macros:

  • Calorie balancing — each food's portions go to whichever meals are currently lowest in calories (weighted by each meal's optional share weight), so load spreads evenly across the day instead of front-loading meal 1.
  • Carbs near the workout — a food whose carbs supply > 40% of its calories is "carb-heavy" and claims the pre-workout slot first, then balances the rest.
  • Respect the splits — the daily total is divided into k portions where k is chosen so each portion fits within the per-meal min/max/step, and foods can be barred from certain named meals (excludedMealNames).
  • Tightest first — foods with the narrowest min-to-max range are placed first to reduce conflicts.

9Why this algorithm?

The current deterministic multi-start local search replaced two earlier approaches. The comparison is the best summary of the design constraints:

REJECTED

Simulated annealing

Worked, but stochastic — regenerating the same plan produced different output, so the committed files churned on every run. Non-determinism is a dealbreaker for checked-in plans.

REJECTED

Exact branch-and-bound

Provably optimal, but seconds-to-minutes per plan. Cutting and bulking zero-weight a macro, creating a vast plateau of equally-good fine-step combinations it had to rule out one at a time.

CHOSEN

Multi-start local search

Deterministic (same input → same output), runs in tens of milliseconds, and hits the exact optimum for most plans — within ~a gram of macros on the rest. A strong heuristic, not a proof, but the right trade.

The one-sentence version. Express "how well does this day match its targets?" as a weighted squared-error score with asymmetric, phase-dependent weights and hard floors (§2–3); enumerate the discrete amounts each food can take (§4); then find the lowest-scoring combination by dropping several smart climbers onto that landscape and letting each walk downhill with whole-range and paired moves (§5–7) — finally, sprinkle the winning daily totals across the day's meals (§8).
